The House Edge of 6-to-5

You can calculate the cost of playing 6-to-5 Blackjack pretty easily. To start, you need to know how frequently you can expect a starting hand of 21 and I promise it's easy to remember.

On average, you will receive a blackjack once every 21 hands.

So estimating the cost of this rule variant becomes pretty easy. Multiply the probability of having a Blackjack (1/21) by the probability of the dealer not having a Blackjack (1 - 1/21) and the change in your payout, which is 0.3 of your initial bet (60% of your half-bet bonus) and you get:

\begin{align} {1 \over 21} \times {20 \over 21} \times 0.3 & \approx 0.0136054 \\ & \approx 1.36054\% \end{align}

And that's a very good approximation for the cost of this rule variant! When you look at the actual cost of the rule, it comes very close:

6-to-5 versus other Rule Variants

So how does 6-to-5 Blackjack stack up against other rule variants and table conditions? Besides the 6-to-5 payout change, the next biggest change would be moving from Single Deck Blackjack to 8 deck Blackjack: 0.47805%. This is almost A THIRD of the penalty for 6-to-5!

Since the number of decks is more of a table condition change, not a rule change, what is the rule change with the largest affect on the house edge? Hitting instead of Standing on a Soft 17 will cost the player 0.19% in house advantage. This makes the 6-to-5 rule more than 6 TIMES WORSE than the next highest rule change.

One aspect of 6-to-5 Blackjack that I almost never hear discussed is the affect on your chip stack. After you receive your first Blackjack, you usually have to start a new stack of oddly colored chips that don't match your main betting stack. For example, if you usually bet $10 per hand, you will usually have all red $5 chips, but when you get a blackjack, now you need to start a new stack of $1 chips because you got paid two $5 red chips and two $1 white chips.

This means that you will often have this weird stack of odd chips that you can't intermingle with your main betting stack. It's not enough to make a full bet, so it just sits there, unspent.

In Real Blackjack, it's pretty easy to keep your chips a consistent color. If you're betting multiples of $10, you'll just receive an odd red $5 chip that you can stack with the rest. Even if you're betting an odd amount (say multiples of $25), you only have to wait until you get a second Blackjack, where your 50% Blackjack bonus will allow you to construct a full bet with your odd chips.

Single Deck

The 6-to-5 payout is often combined with the offering of Single Deck Blackjack. This is the carrot casinos offer before beating you with their stick.

As we have already discussed, the 6-to-5 payout penalty dwarfs the benefit of switching from 6 Deck to Single Deck. Also, when you combine the two rule changes, the 6-to-5 has an even HIGHER penalty, because the main reason why a lower number of decks is beneficial to the player is because you can expect a slightly higher frequency of getting blackjacks. With the lower 6-to-5 payout, the benefits of single-deck are Nerfed.

\begin{align} EV_{single deck} + EV_{6 to 5} & < EV_{singledeck + 6 to 5} \\ 0.45742 + -1.36504 & < -0.93736 \\ -0.90762 & < -0.93736 \\ \end{align}

As you can see, the change in EV is greater than the sum of the two conditions applied independently.
