"Not your Keys? Not your Crypto."
- Literally everyone in crypto

If all of your crypto holdings are on an exchange, do you really own it?

If that exchange gets shut down or if an employee decides to walk away with the company's holdings, how would you get your money back?

The short answer is you won't.

There is little to no protection for a crypto investor with millions on a crypto exchange. The FDIC won't protect you from large losses if the exchange goes under or if they just decide they don't want to honor your deposit.

If you have more than $10,000 worth of crypto, a small $150 investment in a hardware wallet can prevent such a disaster and can offer you peace of mind.

The History of Entrusting Crypto to Others

We're still in the early days of crypto. The term "Wild Wild West" is an extremely appropriate description of the current crypto era.

For ANY cyrpto company that might hold your coins, you are going to be subject to four major risks.

  • Lack of legal expertise
  • Lack of operational expertise
  • Shitty security practices
  • Intentional fraud (Ponzi schemes, Rug pulls, etc.)

If a businessman today decides he wants to build a company that centers around crypto, he is almost certainly a severe risk-taker. This is an EXTREMELY fast-moving sector where things like folliwng laws and ensuring security often fall by the wayside.

It's difficult to find anyone with more than a few months of experience with laws and security in the cyrpto space, so making sure your corportation is protected from both a legal and cybersecurity standpoint is extremely difficult.

What is a Hardware Wallet?

When you hear the term "wallet", you usually think of an object that holds valuables inside. It's true that a hardware wallet protects valuables, but it's not the cryptocurrencies themselves. A crypto wallet doesn't hold crypto. The valuable thing you're holding is the "keys".

I'll say it again:

A crypto wallet doesn't hold crypto.

Picking a Hardware Wallet

There are two major cryptocurrency hardware wallet maufacturers: Trezor and Ledger. If you buy either of their top two products, you'll be fine. The biggest difference between the two is that the Ledger can handle more coins.
