"Not your Keys? Not your Crypto."
- Literally everyone in crypto

If all of your crypto is on an exchange, do you really own it?

If that exchange gets shut down or if an employee decides to walk away with the company's coffer, how would you get your money back?

The short answer is you won't.

There is little to no protection for a crypto investor with their assets in a crypto exchange. The FDIC won't protect you from large losses if the exchange goes under or if they just decide they don't like you and they don't want to honor your deposit.

Why is your crypto in danger?

There are 3 major factors that put your coin in peril.

  • Mismanagement
  • Malice
  • Misconduct

Mismanagement

If you need a great attorney, you have thousands to choose from. If you need a great accountant, there are over a million.

And both of these professions have well established certification processes to ensure they know what they are doing. You can make sure your accountant is a Certified Public Accountant (CPA) and you can make sure your attorney is a memeber of the state bar association.

There really is no recognized certification process for cryptocurrencies. And even the "experts" would only have a few years' worth of experience working with it.

And to top this all off -- nearly EVERYONE involved in crypto is quite comfortable taking on a TON of risk if the rewards involve stratospheric success. This is on top of the risk-taking already associated with technology start-ups.

I've worked with many non-crypto start-ups that sacrifice legal compliance and strong security for the ability to "move fast and break things" as Facebook CEO Mark Zuckerberg so famously put it. Crypto companies take this to a COMPLETELY different level.

Crypto is a new industry.
Very few people have more than 1 or 2 years' of experience with it.
Almost nobody in crypto learned about it in school.
There are no certifications for proving someone is an expert in the field.
Being purely digitadl makes crypto extremely easy to steal.

MtGox.com

Lost 740,000 BTC

In June 2011, the largest Bitcoin exchange was being run from a site originally created to trade cards for the game "Magic: The Gathering" (MTGOX originally stood for "Magic: The Gathering Online eXchange").

There were a series of breaches during that month, including the loss of over 25,000 Bitcoin (then worth around $400k), the posting of their entire customer base to a public site, and the transfer of a "large number" of Bitcoins from a Hacker who compromised an auditor's computer.

Then again in 2014, MtGox announced that they had lost over 740,000+ Bitcoin (then only worth $400M, and in 2022 worth almost $30 Billion). Their customers were never made whole and basically walked away with nothing.

Bitfinex

Lost 120,000 BTC

In 2016, almost 120,000 BTC (then worth around $60M USD and now worth more than $4 BILLION) was stolen from Crypto Exchange site Bitfinex. At the time, this represented about 30% of customers' holdings, and all customers were forced to take the brunt of the loss.

Customers were "compensated" by being issued BFX tokens at a rate of $1 for every dollar lost (30% of each customer's holdings). So if you had $100 of Bitcoin at the time of the breach, you were issued 30 BFX tokens. Within a few months all of those tokens were exchanged for iFinex stock.

If customers were able to hold on to their BTC, they would have seen it appreciate from $600 at the time of the theft to today's price of over $40,000. Instead, they were issued a fraction of their worth in the stock of a shitty company.

StakeHound and Fireblocks

Lost 38,178 ETH

StakeHound promised people that you could "stake" Ethereum with them and earn rewards as they used that Ethereum to create "Validators" to support Ethereum's migration to Proof of Stake.

StakeHound left a third-party company named Fireblocks handle the custody of that ETH, and in June 2021, Fireblocks reportedly lost the private keys to 38,178 ETH (currently worth $110+ Million USD) and did not have a backup.

Not having access to the private keys means that the ETH is unrecoverable. It is unclear whether customers will be compensated for the loss.

Silk Road

144,000+ Bitcoin Seized

In 2013, the one of the largest Crypto sites was being run by a 29 year-old named Ross "Dread Pirate Roberts" Ulbrecht. He ran the Dark Web site "Silk Road", and was worth over $80 million.

He oversaw over $1 BILLION in transactions through his site, which facilitated the sale of illegal drugs, fake IDs, and more illicit items. He got caught after bragging on LinkedIn about how he ran a multi-million dollar drug marketplace.

When the site was shut down in 2013, the FBI seized 144,000+ Bitcoin, including 26,000 of their customers' balances. That cache of Bitcoin was never returned to Silk Road customers, and because of the nature of the illegal purchases they were involved in, I doubt many of those people are going to come forward to stake their claim to their lost coin.

Take your Crypto off the Sites

When you don't have complete control over your crypto, it's subject to being taken from you, and there are many reasons to believe it will be taken.

I've had money taken away from me by PayPal because an eBay customer fraudulently lied to them about a sale that I made and delivered as described.

I know people who have had their Venmo accounts locked because the transaction comments mentioned the word "poker".

I've had my NETELLER account frozen because I moved to a different country and they thought I was trying to commit fraud.

If you entrust a large part of your life savings to a company that isn't safeguarded by established practices and regulatory protections, you shouldn't be surprised if you lose access to it all.

So to prevent yourself being locked out of your funds due to their crappy management, their interpretation of your actions, or straight up thievery, you need to move your crypto off of exchanges and lending sites like BlockFi and Celcius.
